# What is Product–AI Fit

> AI is not inherently valuable. It is only valuable when it solves a problem that couldn't be solved before — or solves an existing problem dramatically better.

## The Problem

Most AI products fail the fit test. They add intelligence without adding value. They bolt AI onto workflows that don't need it, or add novelty without reducing effort.

The question is not "Can we add AI here?" The question is "Does AI create value that justifies the complexity it introduces?"

## What Product–AI Fit Looks Like

Product–AI fit exists when three things are true:

**1. The problem has inherent uncertainty**  
AI is useful when the solution cannot be reduced to fixed rules. If the task can be handled by deterministic logic, AI adds cost without adding value.

**2. The AI output integrates into existing workflows**  
Intelligence in isolation is not useful. The output must feed directly into what users already do, or replace a step that was previously manual and painful.

**3. The reliability threshold is met**  
Users will not tolerate AI that fails unpredictably. If the task requires 99% accuracy and the model produces 85%, the product does not have fit — regardless of how impressive the demo looks.

## Where Fit Breaks Down

**AI for the sake of AI:** Adding a chatbot to a product that already has clear navigation does not create value. It creates a second interface users have to learn.

**Ignoring integration cost:** AI features often require users to copy-paste, manually verify, or rework outputs. If the overhead exceeds the time saved, fit does not exist.

**Over-indexing on capability:** A model that generates brilliant output 80% of the time and nonsense 20% of the time is not a usable product. Capability without reliability is not fit.

## The Test

Ask three questions:

1. **What problem does this solve that could not be solved before?**  
   If the answer is "it's faster" or "it's smarter," that is not enough. Speed and intelligence are not inherently valuable. They are valuable when they unlock something that was previously impossible or prohibitively expensive.

2. **Does the AI output integrate seamlessly into the user's workflow?**  
   If the user has to manually verify, reformat, or fix the output, the friction cost may exceed the value. Fit exists when the output can be trusted and used directly.

3. **Is the reliability threshold acceptable for this use case?**  
   A writing assistant can afford to be 80% accurate because users can edit. A medical diagnosis tool cannot. Fit depends on whether the model's error rate aligns with the use case's tolerance.

## What This Means for Product Decisions

Product–AI fit is not binary. It is a spectrum. A product can have partial fit (valuable but not ready) or conditional fit (valuable for some users, not others).

The PM's job is to identify where fit exists and where it does not — and design accordingly. This means:

- Starting with workflows, not models
- Designing reliability layers, not just intelligence layers
- Measuring value by effort reduced, not features added

## Takeaway

AI is not a feature. It is a capability that either fits the problem or does not.

Most AI products fail because they optimize for intelligence instead of fit.

The best products do the opposite: they start with the problem, identify where AI creates value, and design systems that deliver that value reliably.

That is product–AI fit.
